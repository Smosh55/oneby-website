Ask most home service owners how many calls they miss in a week and you'll get a shrug. Ask them what those missed calls cost and you'll get silence. That silence is the most expensive thing in the business.

A missed call feels like nothing. The phone rang, you were busy, it stopped. But on the other end was a homeowner with a problem and a credit card, and they didn't leave a voicemail — they called the next company on the list. That's not a missed call. That's a missed job.

## The math nobody runs

Let's put real numbers on it. Say your business takes 100 inbound calls in a week and misses 25 of them — a conservative figure for trades where the team is in the field, not by the phone.

- **25 missed calls/week**
- Roughly **half** were potential new jobs: ~12 lost opportunities
- Your average job is worth **$450**
- Your typical close rate on answered calls is **40%**

That's about **5 jobs a week** you never even knew you had a shot at. At $450 each, that's **$2,250 a week** — over **$115,000 a year** walking straight to your competitors.

> The painful part: you already paid to generate most of those calls. Your ads, your truck wraps, your reviews — they did their job. The phone rang. The leak was at the last step.

## Why voicemail doesn't save you

The standard answer is "but they can leave a message." They won't. The overwhelming majority of callers to a small business will not leave a voicemail. They're standing in a flooded kitchen or a sweltering house. They want a human, and if they don't get one in a few seconds, they hang up and dial again.

Even when someone does leave a message, you get this: *"Yeah, hi, it's about my... thing. Call me back."* No name spelled out, no clear problem, no urgency captured. Now your dispatcher is playing phone tag with a half-message instead of booking a job.

## The three hidden costs

The lost job is the obvious cost. There are three quieter ones:

1. **Acquisition waste.** Every missed lead means the money you spent to make the phone ring was burned. Your real cost-per-booked-job is far higher than your cost-per-lead suggests.
2. **Reputation drift.** "I called twice and nobody picked up" becomes a one-star review and a story they tell their neighbor.
3. **Owner burnout.** Many owners "solve" missed calls by never putting the phone down — answering from the ladder, at dinner, on vacation. That's not a system. That's a tax on your life.

## What "answering every call" is actually worth

Flip the math. If you could capture even **half** of those missed opportunities, that's ~$1,100 a week in recovered revenue. The cost of a system that answers, captures the details, and books the job is a small fraction of that.

This is the whole idea behind treating communication as an operating system instead of a phone line. When a call comes in and nobody can pick up, an AI receptionist answers, finds out what the customer needs, and turns it into a task your team can act on — before the customer ever calls your competitor.

The call still happened. The difference is whether it becomes a job or a shrug.

## The takeaway

You don't have a marketing problem if your phone is ringing. You have a capture problem. And unlike most problems in a service business, this one has a clean, measurable fix:

- Count your missed calls for one week.
- Multiply by your average job value and close rate.
- Decide whether that number is acceptable.

For almost every owner who runs that math honestly, it isn't.
