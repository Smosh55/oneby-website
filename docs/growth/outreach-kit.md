## (a) The after-hours call play

The pitch writes itself: we call them after hours, and nobody answers. That missed
call IS the demo.

### Nightly workflow (30–45 min, 8–9pm local time of the target list)

1. Build the night's list earlier in the day: 15–20 businesses in one trade + one
   metro (pull from Google Maps: "hvac repair <city>", sort by reviews 10–200 —
   big enough to afford $29/mo, small enough to have no call center).
2. Log each in a sheet: business name, number, owner name if findable (site/GBP/
   state license lookup), source URL.
3. Between 8–9pm local, call each number once from your cell. Log the outcome:
   - `A` answered by a human
   - `VM` voicemail (note: full box? generic greeting? gives an emergency number?)
   - `NA` rings out / disconnected
   - `SVC` answering service picked up
4. Say nothing misleading if a human answers: "Sorry — calling about a service
   quote, I'll try back tomorrow" and hang up. They answered; they go in the
   follow-up list as a different (weaker) pitch or get skipped.
5. Next morning, 8:30–10am: call every `VM`/`NA` back with the script below.
   Same day: send Email 1 to everyone you couldn't reach by phone.

Rules: one call per business per night, never past 9pm local, mark do-not-contact
immediately if asked. Keep the log — "we called 200 shops after hours and 74% went
to voicemail" becomes content later (real number, from the sheet, or don't say it).

### Morning-after phone script (5 lines)

> 1. "Morning — is this the owner? This is FIRSTNAME, I'll take ten seconds."
> 2. "I called your line at ten of nine last night about a service quote and got
>    voicemail — no callback prompt, nothing. I'm guessing that happens a lot
>    after hours."
> 3. "I'm building OneBy — an AI that answers those calls, gets the address and
>    the problem, and has a job ticket waiting for you in the morning."
> 4. "It's $29 a month locked for our founding members — less than what one missed
>    NIGHT JOB TYPE (e.g. no-cool call / burst pipe) pays you."
> 5. "Can I text you a 2-minute demo link, or is this a bad problem to have?"

Then stop talking. If they say missed calls aren't a problem, thank them and end it
— the list is long.

### Voicemail script (~20 seconds)

> "Hi, this is FIRSTNAME. I called your office at TIME last night and got this same
> voicemail — that's actually why I'm calling. I build OneBy, an AI receptionist
> for TRADE shops: it answers the calls you can't, gets the job details, and texts
> you the summary. If last night's caller was a real job, that math matters. I'm at
> YOUR NUMBER — or don't call back, and I'll email you the short version. Thanks."

## (b) 3-email cold B2B sequence

CAN-SPAM checklist baked in: accurate from-name and subject, physical mailing
address in the footer, working opt-out honored within 10 business days, no
misleading headers. Send from a real person (founder), plain text, no images,
2–4 days between emails. Stop the sequence the moment anyone replies or opts out.

**Footer on every email (required):**

> OneBy · PHYSICAL MAILING ADDRESS, CITY, STATE ZIP
> Don't want emails from me? Reply "no thanks" or click here to unsubscribe:
> UNSUBSCRIBE LINK — takes one click, and I'll never email you again.

## (c) 3 community value-post templates (FB groups / Reddit)

Ground rules: read each group's promo rules first (see targets.md), post from the
personal account, lead with the useful thing, mention OneBy only when asked or
where rules allow a disclosed plug. The calculator link carries
`utm_source=community&utm_medium=organic&utm_campaign=<group-or-sub>`.
Never post the same text twice in one week — groups cross-pollinate.

### Post 1 — the question (no link at all)

Works everywhere, including strict no-promo subs.

> Owners — what actually happens to your phone after 6pm?
>
> Genuinely asking. I've been calling TRADE shops after hours (research for
> something I'm building, full disclosure) and roughly X of Y went straight to
> voicemail with no emergency option. [Use your real logged numbers or cut this
> sentence.]
>
> If you're a one-truck or three-truck shop: do you forward to your cell, eat the
> voicemails, pay an answering service, or just accept that night calls walk?
> And if they walk — do you have any idea what that costs you a month, or is it
> one of those numbers you'd rather not know?

Then answer every reply like a human. If someone asks what you're building, say it
plainly and link once.

### Post 2 — the calculator share (disclosed, for groups that allow tools)

> I built a free calculator for "what do missed calls actually cost me" — sharing
> in case it's useful. Disclosure: I'm building a paid product in this space, but
> the calculator is free, no email wall, no signup.
>
> It asks four things: calls per week, share you miss, share of those that are
> real jobs, close rate, average ticket. It deliberately does NOT assume every
> missed call is a lost job — vendors and wrong numbers don't count, so the
> number stays honest instead of scary.
>
> LINK
>
> If your number comes out near zero, congrats — your phone coverage is fine and
> you shouldn't buy anything from anyone, including me. Curious what ranges people
> get; the spread between one-truck and five-truck shops has been wild.

### Post 3 — the findings post (after ~2 weeks of the nightly play)

Only post real logged numbers.

> We called N TRADE companies in CITY/REGION after hours over the past two weeks.
> Results:
>
> - X% went to voicemail with a generic greeting
> - X% rang out — no voicemail at all
> - X% had a full mailbox
> - X% offered an emergency line or answering service
> - X% — a human picked up
>
> Method: one call per shop, 8–9pm local, weeknights. Not a scientific sample,
> just what happened.
>
> The part that surprised me: [pick the real surprise — full mailboxes, or how
> many rang out]. If a homeowner with water on the floor hits that, they're
> calling the next result on Google.
>
> Disclosure: I'm building an AI answering product, so I have a horse in this
> race — that's why I was making the calls. But the numbers are the numbers.
> Happy to share the sheet.
